# **Self, Uncertainty, and Lenses**

Applied notes on the architecture of experience

This document assumes the lenses from:

* What Appears – a phenomenological sketch of field, structure, compression, hub, narrative, feeling. 

* Appearance, Field, and Compression – a structural model that ties field, world-level, awareness-mode, mind, self, and time together.

* two different algorithms for relating to uncertainty,

## **0.1 Tiny glossary (added notions)**

Alongside the terms from the first two docs (field, structure, compression, awareness-mode, world-level, etc.), we’ll use:

* Stable I / self-compression

   A relatively persistent pattern (narrative \+ body-schema \+ role expectations) that coordinates action: “I, here, this kind of person.” It’s a high-compression construct running in awareness-mode. 

* Uncertainty (experiential)

   The felt openness of possible developments: multiple incompatible future compressions seem live; no single “next stable pattern” has landed yet.

* Anxiety (in this lens)

   Affective signal that:

   current compressions (self, world, time, story) don’t yield a clear or safe enough next move from here. 

* Lens

   A way of organizing and talking about appearance (e.g. “human lens”, “process lens”, “nondual lens”), each with its own defaults and hazards.

* Algorithm / strategy

   A recurring way an awareness-mode handles uncertainty and affect (e.g. “relax identification”, “strengthen and clarify ‘I’”).

## **1\. Very short recap of the base architecture**

From the previous documents, in one breath:

* There is appearance: whatever is present – world, body, thoughts, feelings. 

* The field is a name for the unity of this appearance, taken all-at-once.

* Appearance is structured and relational from the start (near/far, figure/ground, inside/outside, etc.).

* Compression is how appearance shows up more coarsely or finely articulated:

   chunked into “things”, “me”, “time”, “story”, rather than as ungrouped flux.

* World-level: a high-compression, high-differentiation regime where stable objects, space, time, and causal regularities appear – our familiar physical world. 

* Awareness-mode: the field structured as a point of view – a hub that appears as “here / this body / me in this world”, used to predict and act.

* On top of that, further compressions yield:

  * mind (concepts, models),

  * self (identity, narrative),

  * time (ordered story).

Nondual clarity is a way of reading this whole setup without turning “me” and “world” into two separate substances. Differentiation remains; duality relaxes.

## **2\. The “stable I” as self-compression (and why it’s not the enemy)**

Within this architecture, self / ego is:

a highly compressed narrative \+ memory pattern \+ body-schema,  
creating a stable-enough “I” that coordinates action over time. 

This “stable I” is not an extra metaphysical object. It is:

* a world-level pattern: a particular organization of body, brain, environment,

* plus a phenomenological pattern: a recurring way the field groups experiences as “mine / me / for me”.

Functionally, the stable I:

* ties past events together (“my history”),

* projects into possible futures (“what I might do / what might happen to me”),

* tracks commitments and roles,

* carries responsibility (“I did that”, “I want this”),

* provides a handle for explanation: “Why did I act that way?”

From this perspective:

* The goal is not to annihilate the stable I.

* The goal is to debug and refine this self-compression so it is:

  * reality-aligned,

  * flexible,

  * non-delusional,

  * able to act and own its actions,

  * and not so rigid that it fractures under uncertainty.

“Dissolving the I” can be useful as a temporary loosening (seeing that self is not absolute), but as a permanent project it breaks the very interface the system uses to navigate this world-regime.

So we treat:

Self as critical infrastructure,  
not as a cosmic mistake.

## **3\. Uncertainty and anxiety in this architecture**

### **3.1 Uncertainty as open compression**

In world-level terms:

* The field evolves as patterns that are not fully settled yet into a single, stable story:

  * many ways things could compress,

  * not yet known which one will land.

In immediate experience, that shows up as:

* multiple future scenarios,

* competing expectations,

* a felt inability to pick a single “next stable pattern” that feels safe or coherent.

In this lens:

Uncertainty \=  
the field, from this awareness-mode, currently presents multiple live ways of compressing future events, with no single one yet trusted as “how it will actually go”.

### **3.2 Anxiety as a fit signal**

From What Appears, feeling can be read as: 

the felt quality of how the current organization of experience fits or fails to fit what’s happening and what seems possible.

Applied here:

* Anxiety often feels like:

  * “Too many possibilities / too much uncertainty; the ways I’m holding things don’t yield a safe or clear next move.”

So:

Anxiety \=  
one way the system signals that current compressions (world model, self-story, expectations) do not give a workable next action under perceived risk.

It is not “wrong” in itself; it’s a fit / mismatch indicator.

## **4\. Two algorithms for facing uncertainty**

Within this architecture, we can see at least two distinct strategies that an awareness-mode might use.

### **4.1 Algorithm A – Stabilise and clarify the “I”**

Core move:

Don’t try to love pure uncertainty;  
don’t abandon yourself inside it.

Sketch:

1. Stay clear on “my side”

   * Notice: What do I want? What feels okay / not okay?

   * Clarify boundaries and preferences.

2. Track fuzziness

   * If a situation stays too vague, ambiguous, or contradictory for too long –

      notice the ongoing cost (cognitive, emotional, energetic).

3. Adjust investment

   * Reduce or stop investing attention, emotion, or time into situations that remain intolerably fuzzy and unresponsive to clarification.

4. Keep a coherent self-story online

   * Maintain a narrative that can explain your actions:

     * “Given what I knew, and what I wanted, this is why I chose X.”

5. Treat uncertainty as a texture within the field

   * Uncertainty is in experience, not what you are.

   * The “I” is a stable-enough compression that moves through uncertain situations, instead of dissolving into them.

Benefits:

* Preserves agency and accountability.

* Supports practical decision-making under uncertainty.

* Protects against fragmentation and self-abandonment.

Failure modes:

* The stable I can become too rigid:

  * over-controlled,

  * allergic to ambiguity,

  * more focused on coherence than contact with reality.

---

### **4.2 Algorithm B – Relax duality with uncertainty (process lens)**

Core move:

Stop alienating uncertainty as an external threat;  
see it as built into the very process that “you” are.

Sketch:

1. Notice that uncertainty is everywhere

   * At the level of the field, everything is in flux; stability is a compressed read-out. 

2. Re-read self as a changing pattern

   * The “I” is itself unstable: thoughts, feelings, intentions are in motion.

   * Self is a dynamic artifact of ongoing “collapse” (selection among possibilities).

3. Unhook “me vs uncertainty”

   * Instead of:

     * “I, the solid one, am facing external uncertainty,”

   * shift to:

     * “This process, which I call ‘me’, includes uncertainty as one of its textures.”

4. Leave room in every plan

   * Expect that outcomes will deviate.

   * Build slack into expectations and strategies.

5. Act, then respond to collapse

   * Make your best bet,

   * And relate to whatever actually happens as part of the same process, not a betrayal of “how it should have been.”

Benefits:

* Reduces over-identification with fixed narratives and plans.

* Softens the “me vs world” battle around uncertainty.

* Can dramatically lower anxiety by removing an imagined enemy.

Failure modes:

* If taken as a total stance, can:

  * weaken felt agency (“things just happen”),

  * encourage bypass (“it’s all process, no need to look at my specific patterns”),

  * destabilize people whose self-compression is already fragile.

---

### **4.3 A composite strategy**

The two algorithms are not enemies; they are complementary tools:

* Use Algorithm B to:

  * loosen rigid identification,

  * remember that both “world” and “self” are compressions in a larger field,

  * soften the me-vs-uncertainty drama.

* Use Algorithm A to:

  * keep a functional stable I,

  * make concrete decisions,

  * maintain self-respect and boundaries,

  * give honest explanations of your own actions.

In practice, a healthy architecture might:

periodically relax into “this is all process” for de-fragging,  
then return to “I, here, now” to act, explain, and take responsibility.

## **5\. Lenses and psych safety**

Because these ideas operate directly on self and world models, how we talk about them matters.

### **5.1 Lens clash**

We can distinguish at least two lenses:

* Human / structural lens

  * Focus: how experience is organized here-and-now.

  * Questions like:

    * “How do I function under uncertainty?”

    * “How can I stay coherent and not abandon myself?”

  * Concerned with:

    * boundaries,

    * self-regulation,

    * communication impact.

* Process / pointer lens

  * Focus: how everything is part of one process/field.

  * Phrases like:

    * “I am uncertainty,”

    * “everything already happened,”

    * “there is only the field.”

  * Aimed at:

    * dissolving duality,

    * reducing over-attachment,

    * pointing beyond rigid self-world stories.

A lot of confusion happens when:

* a sentence formed in the process lens is heard through the human lens, or vice versa.

Example:

* “I am uncertainty” can be a useful pointer (process lens),

* but structurally incoherent if taken literally as a self-definition inside the human lens that needs clear “I” / “world” distinctions to function.

### **5.2 Safety and context**

Some minds:

* benefit from strong process pointers,

* can integrate “no-self”, “it’s all process”, “everything is connected” without losing functionality.

Other minds:

* are already close to fragmentation,

* may use such pointers to:

  * avoid their own pain,

  * abdicate responsibility (“there is no me, so nothing matters”),

  * or slip into destabilizing interpretations.

So this lens recommends:

* Explicitly naming the lens you’re speaking from.

* Being careful with strong metaphors around:

  * no-self,

  * determinism,

  * “already happened”,

  * “just let go into the field.”

* Treating these as optional tools, not requirements.

You can always fall back to:

“I’m not trying to explain the universe;  
I’m just trying to explain my own actions in a way that feels honest and workable.”

## **6\. Working with this in practice (very short protocols)**

This document is mostly structural, but a few operational snippets follow naturally.

### **When anxiety \+ uncertainty spike**

1. Name the situation

   * “There are too many live possible compressions; my current story doesn’t give a safe next move.”

2. Switch briefly into process lens (Algorithm B)

   * “This is the field in a certain configuration. Uncertainty is not an enemy; it’s part of how this process works.”

3. Return to stable I (Algorithm A)

   * “Given who I am and what I care about, what actually matters here?”

   * “What boundary or preference wants to be honored?”

4. Adjust investment

   * If the situation remains intolerably fuzzy and unresponsive, consciously reduce investment:

     * less time,

     * less emotional charge,

     * fewer future bets.

5. Update self-compression

   * Let the “I” story incorporate this:

     * “In situations like this, I don’t abandon myself; I reduce my stake.”
